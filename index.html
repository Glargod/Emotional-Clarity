The Problem: Ambiguity in Emotional Terminology

In the fields of psychology, sociology, and ethics, the terms 'sympathy' and 'empathy' have long been used interchangeably, leading to significant conceptual and practical challenges. This ambiguity hinders clear communication and effective action in both clinical and societal contexts.

Historically, 'empathy' (derived from the German Einfühlung, meaning 'feeling into') was meant to capture a broad range of emotional and cognitive responses. However, its application has become muddled, often conflating distinct processes.

This lack of precision affects therapeutic interventions, policy-making, and interpersonal relationships, where understanding the nuances of emotional responses is critical.

The Confusion: Overlapping Definitions and Misapplications

The confusion arises from the historical and academic evolution of 'empathy' and 'sympathy.' Originally, 'sympathy' referred to feeling sorrow or concern for another, while 'empathy' was intended to describe understanding another's perspective.

However, psychological research in the late 20th century split 'empathy' into 'cognitive empathy' (understanding) and 'emotional empathy' (sharing feelings), further complicating the terminology.

This split, while academically motivated, has led to widespread misunderstanding. For example, in everyday language, 'empathy' is often used to mean both understanding and feeling, blurring the lines between cognitive and emotional processes.

The result is a lack of clarity in clinical settings, where precise terminology is essential for diagnosing and treating emotional disorders, and in societal contexts, where policy responses to crises require distinct emotional frameworks.

The Solution: Introducing Koinpathy

Scholarly Introduction

Over time, the term empathy has fractured into multiple, often conflicting interpretations — ranging from cognitive understanding to affective resonance and even prosocial motivation. This semantic diffusion has weakened its precision in both academic and therapeutic contexts. To restore clarity and expand emotional taxonomy, Robert Charest (2025) introduces koinpathy — a term derived from the Greek koinos (“shared”) and pathos (“feeling”).

Koinpathy identifies a distinct emotional phenomenon in which individuals do not merely understand or mirror another’s emotions but enter a shared affective state that catalyzes cooperative intent or collective action. It bridges the conceptual gap between empathy’s inward resonance and compassion’s outward expression, defining the emotional alignment that emerges when a group’s awareness becomes unified through shared experience.

This distinction provides a foundation for refining emotional literacy, designing more cohesive social interventions, and re-examining how collective emotional dynamics influence moral behavior and social cohesion.

Sympathy

"The self-emotional response to an outside event. It is the internal feeling of concern or sorrow triggered by another person's situation."

Example: "I feel sorry for your loss."

Empathy

"The awareness that an outside event created an outside emotion. It is the cognitive recognition of another person's emotional state without necessarily sharing it."

Example: "I understand why you feel devastated."

Koinpathy

koinpathy

Pronunciation: /ˈkɔɪnˌpæθi/

Etymology: From Ancient Greek koinos (κοινός, “shared, common”) + pathos (πάθος, “feeling, suffering”). Coined by Robert Charest (2025).

Definition:

  1. A shared emotional state among individuals that generates a sense of mutual identification and collective responsibility for one another’s wellbeing.
  2. An evolved form of empathy in which understanding and feeling another’s experience are accompanied by an intrinsic impulse toward communal action or cooperative alignment.

Distinctions:

  • Sympathy expresses concern for another’s feelings.
  • Empathy conveys understanding or vicarious feeling of those emotions.
  • Koinpathy embodies shared experience and cooperative response, transforming awareness into collective intent or action.

Example sentences:

  • The disaster relief teams acted out of koinpathy, not obligation — they truly shared the victims’ grief and worked as one body to heal it.
  • Koinpathy in a classroom fosters unity; students don’t just relate to each other’s struggles, they move together toward solutions.

Related forms: koinpathic (adj.), koinpathically (adv.)

Implications and Applications: Enhancing Clarity in Practice

This new framework has profound implications for clinical practice, policy-making, and interpersonal relationships. By distinguishing between these terms, we can better address emotional and social challenges.

In therapy, understanding whether a patient's response is sympathetic, empathetic, or koinpathic can guide interventions. For example, group therapy might focus on fostering koinpathy to enhance collective healing.

In policy, recognizing koinpathy can lead to more effective community responses to crises, ensuring that shared emotional experiences translate into actionable support.

Interpersonally, this framework encourages clearer communication about emotional states, reducing misunderstandings and fostering stronger connections.
